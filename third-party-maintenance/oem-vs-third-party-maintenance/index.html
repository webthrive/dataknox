What OEM Maintenance Actually Includes

OEM maintenance contracts like Cisco SmartNet, HPE Foundation Care, and Dell ProSupport are valuable — when hardware is new and software updates matter. As hardware ages, the calculus shifts. You're paying premium pricing for parts and labor coverage that a third-party provider can match at 30–85% less. The question is which OEM contract features you actually use — and which you're paying for unnecessarily.

Decision Framework

When OEM Makes Sense — When TPM Does

When OEM Makes Sense

New Hardware in Warranty

Warranty-period OEM coverage is typically bundled or low-cost. No financial reason to switch until the warranty expires.

Active Software Development

If your environment depends on OS, firmware, or security updates from the OEM, the software access included in OEM contracts has real value.

Regulatory OEM Requirement

Some compliance frameworks or internal policies require OEM-backed support. Verify this requirement is real before assuming it applies.

Mission-Critical New Deployments

For brand-new infrastructure within the first 1–2 years, OEM familiarity and escalation paths have practical value.

When TPM Makes Sense

Hardware 3+ Years Old

Once servers, storage, or networking are past the 3-year mark, OEM pricing typically no longer reflects actual failure risk or support cost.

Approaching or Past EoL

OEM EoL declarations don't reflect actual hardware end-of-life. TPM provides coverage continuity without forced refresh.

Refresh Timeline Delayed

Capital budgets shift. TPM fills the 12–36 month gap between OEM support expiry and an approved hardware refresh cycle.

Mixed-OEM Environment

Managing Dell, HPE, Cisco, and IBM contracts separately is expensive and operationally complex. TPM consolidates to one contract.
